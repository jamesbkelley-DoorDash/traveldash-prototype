Third mode, same homescreen

TravelDash lives alongside Offer and By Time as a third earning mode in the bottom sheet. The mode selector uses the same tab pattern Dashers already know from Monarch.

The purple accent distinguishes TravelDash from Offer (red) and By Time (blue), signaling it's a different kind of earning.

DESIGN NOTE: Uses existing Monarch bottom-sheet pattern. Mode tabs match the Dash-Panel component spec from RFC 2027. TravelDash doesn't replace anything — it adds a third option.
PRIOR ART: "Dash Along the Way" (2023) and "End of Dash Destination" tested similar concepts with positive signals. TravelDash extends both into a full mode.

Two decisions, that's it

Where are you going? Pick from saved places (Home, Work) or search. The map previews your route with a purple corridor showing the delivery matching zone.

How much time can you spare? Pill selector for time tolerance per delivery. Simple, no math. The system estimates how many deliveries might match along your route.

The key insight: we show "Potential deliveries along route" so the Dasher has realistic expectations before starting. If the route has low density, they see "0–1" and can decide it's not worth it.

DESIGN NOTE: The purple corridor on the map is the matching zone — any store/delivery pair within this corridor that fits the time budget becomes a candidate. Wider tolerance = wider corridor.
The time tolerance selector avoids Van Westendorp complexity. Research should validate whether 5/10/15/20 are the right increments or if a continuous slider is better.

The Dasher drives toward their destination normally. The purple route line shows their path, and faded red dots represent restaurants the system is monitoring along the corridor.

DESIGN NOTE: This is the zero-commitment state. If no orders match, the Dasher just arrives at their destination. No wasted time, no frustration. This is what makes TravelDash fundamentally different from a shift.

The magic moment

An order comes in that fits the Dasher's route and time budget. The map shows the detour: current position → store pickup → customer drop-off → back to route.

The order card leads with what matters most: "On your route • adds ~8 min". Then pay, time added, and distance. The Dasher can see at a glance whether this detour is worth it.

"Skip" doesn't end TravelDash — the system keeps scanning for the next match. Zero pressure.

DESIGN NOTE: The "+8 min added time" is the key differentiator from a normal offer. In standard Offer Mode you see pay/distance/items. In TravelDash you see pay/time-added/distance because the question isn't "is this a good delivery?" — it's "is this worth the detour?"
RISK: Store wait time estimation is the killer variable. "~3 min wait" must be conservative. A Chipotle at noon might actually be 15 min. If the estimate is wrong once, trust in the feature dies.

Delivery + route awareness

The delivery screen looks like a standard delivery — same drop-off instructions, same complete button. But it adds one crucial element: "Back on route to Home in ~3 min after drop-off."

This reminds the Dasher they're still going somewhere. The delivery is a stop along the way, not a detour into the unknown. The green route line shows the path to the customer, and the faded purple line shows how they'll rejoin their original route.

DESIGN NOTE: After completing this delivery, the system can either (a) resume scanning for the next match, or (b) show a "Continue to Home" confirmation if they're close to destination. The "just one more" problem means we should default to asking rather than auto-scanning.

The payoff screen

When the Dasher arrives at their destination, TravelDash shows what they earned along the way. The key metrics reinforce the value proposition:

"0 dead miles" — every mile was either going home or earning money. No driving to a zone, no wasted trips.

"$18.90/hr effective" — even though per-delivery pay was normal, the effective hourly rate is high because there were zero wasted miles. This is the economic reframe.

"Accuracy vs estimate: Within 2 min" — building trust in the system's predictions. If this is consistently accurate, Dashers will keep using TravelDash.

DESIGN NOTE: This screen subtly normalizes "Orders skipped: 1" — reinforcing that skipping is fine, there's no penalty, and TravelDash is truly zero-pressure.
